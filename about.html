About This Project

A journey through the animals of the land, mountains, and sea.

Exploring Life on Land

Land animals are some of the most diverse creatures on Earth. From powerful predators to small but important insects, each species has its own way of surviving in forests, deserts, grasslands, and jungles.

This project highlights how these animals live, adapt, and shape the environments around them, giving us a deeper appreciation for the wildlife that shares our planet.

Life in the Mountains and the Sea

Mountain and ocean animals thrive in some of the world’s toughest environments. Whether climbing steep cliffs or navigating deep waters, these species show incredible strength and unique adaptations.

This project celebrates the wildlife that survives in high peaks, cold seas, and everything in between, revealing the remarkable ways animals adapt to extreme habitats.

Wild Cats on Land

Wild cats like lions, tigers, leopards, and jaguars are some of the most powerful predators on land. They shape ecosystems by keeping prey populations balanced and healthy.

Many wild cat species are now endangered because of habitat loss, illegal hunting, and conflict with humans.

Goats in the Mountains

Mountain goats and wild relatives like the ibex and markhor are built for steep cliffs and rocky slopes. Their strong legs and balance help them survive where few animals can.

Some mountain goat species are threatened by climate change, shrinking habitats, and hunting.

Sharks in the Water

Sharks have lived in Earth’s oceans for millions of years and play a key role in keeping marine life balanced. From fast hunters to gentle giants like whale sharks, they are essential to ocean health.

Many shark species are endangered due to overfishing, pollution, and habitat destruction.

These Animals Are at Risk

Whether on land, in the mountains, or in the sea, these animals face growing dangers. Human expansion, climate change, and illegal wildlife trade are pushing many species toward extinction.

Protecting their habitats and reducing human impact is crucial for their survival.
